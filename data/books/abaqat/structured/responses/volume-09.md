# Abaqat al-Anwar — Volume 9 Response Analysis

**Hadith defended:** Hadith of Ghadir Khumm
**Section:** Dalalah (Meaning and Implication)
**Analyzed by:** Gemini 3.1 Pro

## Overview
This volume focuses on the Dalalah (semantic and contextual implication) of the Hadith of Ghadir. The author systematically refutes Fakhr al-Razi and Shah Abdul Aziz Dehlavi's linguistic argument that 'Mawla' cannot mean 'Awla' (more rightful/superior). He then proves the contextual link of Ghadir to Imamate by demonstrating through numerous Sunni sources that the Verse of Tabligh (5:67), the Verse of Ikmal (5:3), and the Verse of Sa'ala Sa'il (70:1) were all revealed regarding Ali at Ghadir Khumm. Finally, he cites the poetry of Hassan b. Thabit and Qays b. Sa'd to show that the early companions explicitly understood the Prophet's declaration of 'Mawla' to mean 'Imam' and 'Hadi'.

## Responses to Dehlavi (4)

### Response 1: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: Shia say: The word 'Mawla' means 'Awla bi al-Tasarruf' (the one with the most right to rule/administer). Therefore, Ali is the designated Caliph. Dehlavi's attack: He accepts the hadith as authentic but completely rejects the Shia linguistic and contextual interpretation.
**Type:** LINGUISTIC_ANALYSIS
**Author's response:** The author refutes the linguistic objection (originating from Fakhr al-Razi) that synonyms must be perfectly interchangeable in all grammatical constructs (e.g., Razi argued you cannot say 'Huwa Mawla min Fulan' instead of 'Awla min Fulan'). The author cites Sunni linguists and Usul scholars to prove that synonyms do not require absolute interchangeability due to specific Arabic usage rules. He lists numerous Sunni exegetes and linguists (Abu Ubaydah, Al-Farra, Al-Zajjaj, Al-Akhfash) who explicitly interpreted 'Mawla' as 'Awla'.
**Evidence:** Al-Radi's Sharh al-Kafiyah, Al-Suyuti's Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir, Statements of Abu Ubaydah, Al-Farra, Al-Zajjaj, Al-Akhfash, Al-Bukhari's Sahih (Kitab al-Fara'id)

### Response 2: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: He accepts the hadith as authentic but completely rejects the Shia linguistic and contextual interpretation.
**Type:** COUNTER_CITATION
**Author's response:** The author proves the contextual connection of Ghadir to Imamate by citing Sunni consensus and narrations that the Verse of Tabligh (5:67) was revealed commanding the Prophet to declare Ali's Wilayah, and the Verse of Ikmal (5:3) was revealed immediately after the declaration, signifying the completion of the religion through Imamate.
**Evidence:** Al-Suyuti's Al-Durr al-Manthur, Al-Wahidi's Asbab al-Nuzul, Al-Tha'labi's Al-Kashf wa al-Bayan, Narrations from Ibn Abi Hatim, Ibn Mardawayh, and Ibn Asakir, Al-Nisaburi's Ghara'ib al-Quran

### Response 3: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: He accepts the hadith as authentic but completely rejects the Shia linguistic and contextual interpretation.
**Type:** COUNTER_CITATION
**Author's response:** The author cites Sunni sources proving that the Verse of 'Sa'ala Sa'il' (70:1) was revealed when Al-Harith b. al-Nu'man al-Fihri objected to the Prophet's declaration of Ali's Wilayah at Ghadir and asked God to send a stone upon him if it was truly from God.
**Evidence:** Al-Tha'labi's Tafsir, Sibt Ibn al-Jawzi's Tadhkirat al-Khawass, Al-Zarandi's Durar al-Simtayn, Al-Samhudi's Jawahir al-Iqdayn

### Response 4: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: He accepts the hadith as authentic but completely rejects the Shia linguistic and contextual interpretation.
**Type:** COUNTER_CITATION
**Author's response:** The author cites the poetry of Hassan b. Thabit recited at Ghadir with the Prophet's approval, and the poetry of Qays b. Sa'd at Siffin, both explicitly using the words 'Imam' and 'Hadi' as the direct meaning of the Prophet's declaration 'Mawla'. This proves the companions understood it as political and spiritual leadership.
**Evidence:** Al-Khawarizmi's Manaqib, Al-Hamawini's Fara'id al-Simtayn, Al-Ganji al-Shafi'i's Kifayat al-Talib, Al-Suyuti's Al-Izdihar

## Biographical Dossiers (9)

- **Ibn Abi Hatim al-Razi** — Highly praised as a leading Imam, Hafiz, and critic of Hadith. His Tafsir is considered one of the most authentic, as he committed to only including sound reports.
  Sources: Siyar A'lam al-Nubala, Tadhkirat al-Huffaz, Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyyah, Tabaqat al-Huffaz
- **Ibn Mardawayh** — Acknowledged as a great Hafiz, historian, and reliable authority in Rijal and Hadith.
  Sources: Tadhkirat al-Huffaz, Al-Ibar fi Khabar man Ghabar, Tabaqat al-Huffaz
- **Abu Nu'aym al-Isfahani** — Described as a famous Hafiz, one of the greatest scholars of Hadith, and author of Hilyat al-Awliya.
  Sources: Wafayat al-A'yan, Al-Wafi bi al-Wafayat, Al-Ikmal fi Asma' al-Rijal
- **Al-Wahidi** — Recognized as the Imam of his time in Tafsir, grammar, and linguistics, and author of the famous Asbab al-Nuzul.
  Sources: Al-Kamil fi al-Tarikh, Siyar A'lam al-Nubala, Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyyah, Wafayat al-A'yan
- **Al-Tha'labi** — Praised as the unique scholar of his time in Tafsir, highly reliable in transmission, and praised by Al-Qushayri.
  Sources: Wafayat al-A'yan, Al-Ibar fi Khabar man Ghabar, Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyyah, Mir'at al-Jinan
- **Ibn Asakir** — Revered as the Hafiz of the Levant, a massive authority in history and Hadith, and author of Tarikh Dimashq.
  Sources: Mu'jam al-Udaba, Wafayat al-A'yan, Tadhkirat al-Huffaz, Mir'at al-Jinan
- **Badr al-Din al-Ayni** — Highly respected Hanafi Chief Justice, historian, and author of Umdat al-Qari (commentary on Sahih al-Bukhari).
  Sources: Al-Daw' al-Lami', Husn al-Muhadarah, Bughyat al-Wu'at, Kashf al-Zunun
- **Al-Samhudi** — Respected as the Mufti and historian of Medina, a great Shafi'i scholar, and author of Wafa' al-Wafa and Jawahir al-Iqdayn.
  Sources: Al-Daw' al-Lami', Al-Nur al-Safir
- **Shahr b. Hawshab** — Defended against Ibn Kathir's weakening; shown to be authenticated by Ahmad b. Hanbal, Yahya b. Ma'in, Al-Bukhari, and Al-Tirmidhi.
  Sources: Al-Kamal fi Ma'rifat al-Rijal, Tahdhib al-Tahdhib, Mizan al-Itidal

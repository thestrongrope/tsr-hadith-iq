## Chapter 7 Internal Structure

### Part I: Sunni Doctrines on Imamate (عقائد — lines 1-464)

Dehlavi first establishes what Sunnis believe, contrasted with Shia positions:

| Doctrine | Line | Content |
|----------|------|---------|
| عقیده دوم | 282 | Imam must be visible, not hidden (مختفی) — attacks occultation |
| عقیده سوم | 333 | Imam need not be infallible (معصوم) in knowledge and ijtihad |
| عقیده چهارم | 412 | Imam need not be divinely designated (منصوص) by God |
| عقیده ششم | 464 | Abu Bakr is the rightful first Imam after the Prophet — by consensus |

### Part II: Proofs for Abu Bakr's Caliphate (lines 464-1453)

Dehlavi presents Sunni proofs from Quran and Ahl al-Bayt traditions supporting the caliphate of Abu Bakr, including extended discussion of the Saqifa event and related hadith.

### Part III: Shia Proofs for Ali's Imamate — The Target of Abaqat (lines 1453-3826)

At line 1453, Dehlavi introduces the three categories of Shia evidence he will refute:

> پس يا از قبیل آیات قرانی خواهند بود یا احادیث متفق علیه یا دلایل عقلیه
> "These [proofs] will either be Quranic verses, or agreed-upon hadiths, or rational arguments"

He calls these اقسام ثلثه (three types), and addresses them in order.

### Dehlavi's Refutation Strategies for Verses

For the **Verse of Wilayah** (his longest refutation, ~250 lines):
1. If this verse proves Ali's exclusive Imamate, it also negates the Imamate of the 11 other Imams (self-defeating for Twelvers)
2. The word ولی is مشترک (polysemous) — could mean helper, friend, or authority
3. The context (سیاق و سباق) points to "helper/friend," not "ruler"
4. The story of Ali giving his ring in ruku' comes only from al-Tha'labi, whom Sunni hadith scholars reject
5. Other causes of revelation (اسباب النزول) exist — e.g., about Abdullah ibn Salam or about fighters against apostates
6. The plural forms (الذین، یقیمون، هم) cannot refer to one person

## Dehlavi's Rhetorical Methodology — Critical Assessment

### Strategy: Ilzam (الزام — Binding by Opponent's Own Sources)

Dehlavi's signature technique is citing Shia sources against Shia positions. He relies heavily on **Nahj al-Balagha** as a weapon, claiming it contains Ali's praise of Abu Bakr and acknowledgment of the Caliphs' legitimacy.

### Case Study: The "Fulan" Passage in Nahj al-Balagha (lines 1088-1137)

Dehlavi quotes what he claims is Ali's praise of Abu Bakr from Nahj al-Balagha:

> للّه بلاد ابی بکر فاحد قوم الاود و داوی العمد و اقام السنة و خلف البدعة ذهب نقی الثوب قلیل العیب

"By God, how excellent was Abu Bakr! He straightened the crooked, healed the wound, established the Sunnah, left behind innovation, departed with clean garments and few faults..."

**Critical problems with this citation:**

1. **The name "Abu Bakr" does not appear in Nahj al-Balagha.** Dehlavi himself admits (line 1091-1092) that the compiler used the word **"فلان" (so-and-so)** instead. He claims this was a deliberate removal:

   > صاحب نهج البلاغة ... برای حفظ مذهب خود تصرفی کرده لفظ ابوبکر را حذف نموده و بجای او لفظ فلان آورده
   > "The compiler of Nahj al-Balagha made a modification to protect his sect — he removed the word 'Abu Bakr' and replaced it with 'fulan'"

2. **He provides zero evidence for this tampering claim.** No manuscript comparison, no earlier source with the name intact, no chain of transmission. It is pure assertion.

3. **He misidentifies the compiler.** He says "صاحب نهج البلاغة که شریف مرتضی است" — attributing the compilation to **Sharif al-Murtada**, when it was actually his brother **Sharif al-Radi** (d. 406 AH).

4. **He cites no specific Shia commentator by name.** His claim that "most Shia commentators" agree the passage refers to Abu Bakr uses only unnamed attribution (line 1094-1096):

   > شارحین نهج البلاغة از امامیه ... **بعضی** گفته اند مراد ابوبکر است و **بعضی** گفته اند عمر است و **اکثر شراح** اول را ترجیح داده اند

   "Commentators from the Imamiyyah ... **some** said it means Abu Bakr, **some** said Umar, and **most commentators** preferred the first."

   No names. No book references. No citations. Just "some...some...most."

5. **He acknowledges and dismisses the Shia counter-argument** (lines 1110-1114): that Ali said these things as political rhetoric (استجلاب قلوب — winning hearts) to manage subjects who revered the Caliphs. Dehlavi calls this unacceptable because it would mean "attributing ten lies confirmed by oath to an infallible Imam."

### Second Case: Ali's Letter to Muawiyah (lines 1404-1414)

Dehlavi cites a letter from Ali to Muawiyah containing:

> لعمری ان مکانهما من الاسلام لعظیم و ان المصاب بهما لجرح فی الاسلام شدید رحمهما الله
> "By my life, their [Abu Bakr and Umar's] position in Islam is great, and the loss of them is a severe wound in Islam, may God have mercy on them"

He then claims (line 1410) that Nahj al-Balagha's compiler tampered with this letter too:

> صاحب نهج البلاغة نیز آورده لیکن درانجا داد تحریف داده مقدم را موخر و موخر را مقدم نموده و آنچه منافی مذهب خود یافته ساقط کرده

"The compiler of Nahj al-Balagha included it but tampered with it — making the beginning the end and the end the beginning, and dropping what conflicted with his sect."

Again he claims "all commentators have acknowledged" (جمیع شارحین اعتراف نموده اند) this tampering — **without naming a single one**.

### Assessment of Dehlavi's Source Criticism

| Claim | Evidence Provided |
|-------|-------------------|
| Al-Radi removed "Abu Bakr" from Nahj al-Balagha | None — assertion only |
| "Most Shia commentators" agree fulan = Abu Bakr | No commentator named |
| Ali's letter to Muawiyah was tampered with by al-Radi | No commentator named, no manuscript evidence |
| Compiler is "Sharif al-Murtada" | **Factually wrong** — it was Sharif al-Radi |

This pattern — making sweeping claims about Shia sources while citing no specific authorities — is precisely the kind of argument that the author of Abaqat al-Anwar would address through his exhaustive biographical dossier method: going to the actual commentaries, quoting them verbatim, and showing whether Dehlavi's characterization is accurate.

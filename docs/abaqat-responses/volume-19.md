# Abaqat al-Anwar — Volume 19 Response Analysis

**Hadith defended:** Hadith of Thaqalayn
**Section:** Sanad
**Analyzed by:** Gemini 3.1 Pro

## Overview
This volume focuses on defending the Sanad (chain of transmission) and the exact wording of the Hadith of Thaqalayn. The author compiles an exhaustive list of eminent Sunni scholars, hadith masters, and exegetes who have narrated, authenticated, and relied upon this hadith. He meticulously builds biographical dossiers for these scholars to prove their high status in Sunni Islam. Furthermore, he dedicates a significant portion of the text to refuting the attempts by Ibn al-Jawzi and Ibn Taymiyyah to weaken the hadith or its specific wordings (such as 'my progeny' and 'they shall not separate'), using the statements of other major Sunni authorities against them.

## Responses to Dehlavi (1)

### Response 1: Hadith of Thaqalayn: Shia say: The Prophet ordered the Ummah to hold onto the Ahl al-Bayt, proving they are the Imams. Dehlavi's attack: Accepts the hadith but argues Sunnis are its true practitioners.
**Type:** SANAD_DEFENSE | CONTRADICTION_EXPOSURE | REBUTTAL_LINEAGE | COUNTER_CITATION
**Author's response:** While Dehlavi accepts the hadith in principle, the author addresses the root of Sunni polemics against the Shia interpretation by defending the absolute authenticity of the specific wording 'my progeny, my Ahl al-Bayt' and 'they shall never separate until they meet me at the Pond'. The author proves these phrases are mass-transmitted and authenticated by the greatest Sunni hadith masters (like Muslim, Tirmidhi, and Hakim). He systematically dismantles the attempts by Ibn al-Jawzi and Ibn Taymiyyah to weaken these specific binding phrases, showing that their weakening contradicts the consensus of Sunni hadith scholars and has been explicitly rejected by authorities like Sakhawi, Samhudi, and Ibn Hajar al-Makki. He even cites al-Jahiz, a known anti-Shia polemicist, who used the full wording of the hadith to prove the superiority of the Ahl al-Bayt.
**Evidence:** Sahih Muslim, Sunan al-Tirmidhi, Al-Mustadrak 'ala al-Sahihayn by al-Hakim, Istijlab Irtiqa' al-Ghuraf by al-Sakhawi, Jawahir al-'Iqdayn by al-Samhudi, Al-Sawa'iq al-Muhriqah by Ibn Hajar al-Makki, Risalat Madh Bani Hashim by al-Jahiz

## Biographical Dossiers (10)

- **Jamal al-Din al-Ifriqi (Ibn Manzur)** — Highly praised as a master of Arabic language, literature, and history; author of Lisan al-Arab; recognized for his vast knowledge and numerous summaries of major works.
  Sources: Al-Wafi bi al-Wafayat, Fawat al-Wafayat, Al-Durar al-Kaminah, Bughyat al-Wu'at, Abjad al-'Ulum
- **Shams al-Din al-Dhahabi** — Revered as the seal of the hadith masters, a brilliant critic of chains of transmission, and the ultimate authority in the science of Rijal and Islamic history.
  Sources: Al-Badr al-Tali', Taj Mukallal, Ithaf al-Nubala'
- **Sharaf al-Din al-Tibi** — Celebrated as a famous Imam, a master of rational and transmitted sciences, rhetoric, and exegesis; known for his piety, wealth spent in charity, and his indispensable commentary on Mishkat al-Masabih.
  Sources: Al-Durar al-Kaminah, Bughyat al-Wu'at, Tabaqat al-Mufassirin, Al-Badr al-Tali', Taj Mukallal
- **Ibn Kathir al-Dimashqi** — Acknowledged as a leading scholar, hafiz, historian, and exegete; highly praised for his exceptional memory, mastery of hadith texts and chains, and his monumental works in history and tafsir.
  Sources: Tabaqat al-Mufassirin, Abjad al-'Ulum
- **Nur al-Din al-Haythami** — Highly respected hafiz and companion of al-Iraqi; praised for his immense memorization of hadith texts, his piety, and his monumental work Majma' al-Zawa'id.
  Sources: Al-Daw' al-Lami', Tabaqat al-Huffaz, Husn al-Muhadarah, Al-Badr al-Tali', Ithaf al-Nubala'
- **Majd al-Din al-Firuzabadi** — Renowned lexicographer and author of Al-Qamus al-Muhit; celebrated for his vast travels, immense knowledge of language and hadith, and high status among kings and scholars.
  Sources: Ithaf al-Nubala'
- **Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani** — Considered the unparalleled master of his time in rational sciences, rhetoric, logic, and theology; his works became standard textbooks across the Islamic world.
  Sources: Tabaqat al-Mufassirin, Al-Badr al-Tali', Abjad al-'Ulum, Taj Mukallal
- **Shams al-Din al-Sakhawi** — A leading hafiz and historian; the author provides a massive, detailed autobiography from Sakhawi's own work to prove his unparalleled mastery in hadith, his vast number of teachers, and his prolific authorship.
  Sources: Al-Daw' al-Lami'
- **Shihab al-Din al-Qastallani** — Respected scholar, hadith master, and author of Irshad al-Sari (commentary on Bukhari) and Al-Mawahib al-Ladunniyyah; known for his piety and mastery of Qira'at.
  Sources: Al-Badr al-Tali'
- **Shihab al-Din al-Khafaji** — Chief Judge and leading Hanafi scholar of Egypt; celebrated for his unmatched eloquence, mastery of Arabic literature, and his valuable commentaries on Tafsir al-Baydawi and Al-Shifa.
  Sources: Khulasat al-Athar, Taj Mukallal

## Argument Lineages Traced (1)

- **The Hadith of Thaqalayn, specifically the wording 'they shall not separate' and the command to hold fast to the Ahl al-Bayt, is weak or fabricated.**
  Ibn al-Jawzi (d. 597) -> Ibn Taymiyyah (d. 728)

## Prior Rebuttals Cited (4)

- **Shams al-Din al-Sakhawi** in *Istijlab Irtiqa' al-Ghuraf*: Refuted Ibn al-Jawzi's inclusion of the Hadith of Thaqalayn in his book of weak/fabricated hadiths (Al-Ilal al-Mutanahiyah), expressing astonishment at his claim that it is not authentic when it is recorded in Sahih Muslim and other major collections.
- **Nur al-Din al-Samhudi** in *Jawahir al-'Iqdayn*: Refuted Ibn al-Jawzi's weakening of the hadith, warning readers not to be deceived by him and pointing out his negligence of the hadith's numerous authentic chains.
- **Ibn Hajar al-Makki** in *Al-Sawa'iq al-Muhriqah*: Refuted Ibn al-Jawzi's claim, stating it was a result of illusion or negligence regarding the hadith's many authentic chains, including the one in Sahih Muslim.
- **Abd al-Ra'uf al-Munawi** in *Fayd al-Qadir*: Explicitly stated that whoever claims the hadith is weak, like Ibn al-Jawzi, is suffering from an illusion (wahm).

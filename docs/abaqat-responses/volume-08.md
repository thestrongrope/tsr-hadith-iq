# Abaqat al-Anwar — Volume 8 Response Analysis

**Hadith defended:** Hadith of Ghadir Khumm
**Section:** Dalalah (Implication/Meaning)
**Analyzed by:** Gemini 3.1 Pro

## Overview
This volume focuses entirely on the semantic implication (Dalalah) of the Hadith of Ghadir Khumm. It systematically refutes Dehlavi's claim that the word 'Mawla' cannot mean 'Awla' (having more right/authority). The author compiles an exhaustive list of 42 prominent Sunni linguists, grammarians, and exegetes who explicitly interpreted 'Mawla' as 'Awla' in the Quran and Hadith. He provides detailed biographical dossiers to establish their supreme authority in the Arabic language, thereby proving that Dehlavi's claim of a 'unanimous rejection' by Arabic linguists is a fabrication. The author also refutes Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's grammatical objections and exposes contradictions in Sunni polemics regarding the meaning of the hadith.

## Responses to Dehlavi (4)

### Response 1: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: Dehlavi accepts the hadith as authentic but completely rejects the Shia linguistic and contextual interpretation. Specifically, he claims that Arabic linguists unanimously deny that 'Mawla' can mean 'Awla' (having more right to rule).
**Type:** LINGUISTIC_ANALYSIS
**Author's response:** The author proves this is a blatant fabrication by listing 42 of the most authoritative Sunni linguists, grammarians, and Quranic exegetes (such as Al-Farra, Abu Ubaydah, Al-Akhfash, Tha'lab, Al-Mubarrad, Al-Jawhari, and Al-Zamakhshari) who explicitly state in their lexicons and Tafsirs that 'Mawla' means 'Awla'.
**Evidence:** Tafsir of Muhammad b. Sa'ib al-Kalbi, Mafatih al-Ghayb by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (quoting Abu Ubaydah and Al-Farra), Al-Sihah by Al-Jawhari, Al-Kashshaf by Al-Zamakhshari, Anwar al-Tanzil by Al-Baydawi

### Response 2: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: Dehlavi claims that only Abu Zayd al-Lughawi allowed 'Mawla' to mean 'Awla', and that the majority of Arabic scholars refuted him for this.
**Type:** BIOGRAPHICAL_DOSSIER
**Author's response:** The author demonstrates that Abu Zayd was not alone, as dozens of other leading scholars held the exact same view. Furthermore, he builds a massive biographical dossier on Abu Zayd al-Ansari, proving from Sunni Rijal sources that he was considered the supreme authority in the Arabic language, superior even to Al-Asma'i and Abu Ubaydah in his breadth of vocabulary, and was never 'refuted' by the majority for this linguistic stance.
**Evidence:** Al-Muzhir by Al-Suyuti, Tahdhib al-Asma wa al-Lughat by Al-Nawawi, Wafayat al-A'yan by Ibn Khallikan, Mir'at al-Janan by Al-Yafi'i

### Response 3: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: Dehlavi (copying Fakhr al-Din al-Razi) argues that grammatically, the morphological pattern 'Maf'al' (مفعل) cannot carry the meaning of the comparative 'Af'al' (أفعل).
**Type:** LINGUISTIC_ANALYSIS
**Author's response:** The author refutes this grammatical objection by showing that the Arabic language contains many anomalous words and usages that lack direct morphological parallels but are nonetheless valid and established by transmission (Sama') from the Arabs. He cites numerous examples of such unique linguistic phenomena accepted by major grammarians, proving that Razi's strict rational-morphological restriction violates actual Arabic usage.
**Evidence:** Al-Ashbah wa al-Naza'ir by Al-Suyuti, Al-Muzhir by Al-Suyuti, Al-Sihah by Al-Jawhari

### Response 4: Hadith of Ghadir Khumm: Dehlavi argues that 'Mawla' means 'Nasir' (helper) or 'Muhibb' (friend), and that Abu Bakr and Umar understood it this way when they congratulated Ali.
**Type:** CONTRADICTION_EXPOSURE
**Author's response:** The author exposes the internal contradictions of Sunni polemicists. He quotes Ibn Hajar al-Haytami's Al-Sawa'iq al-Muhriqah, where Ibn Hajar admits that 'Mawla' can mean 'Awla bil-Ittiba' (most worthy of being followed) or 'Mutasarrif fil-Amr' (administrator of affairs). The author also cites other authentic versions of the Ghadir hadith found in Sunni sources that explicitly use the unambiguous phrases 'Awla bihi min nafsihi' (has more right over him than himself) or 'Waliyyuhu', which serve as an internal commentary (Tafsir) on the word 'Mawla'.
**Evidence:** Al-Sawa'iq al-Muhriqah by Ibn Hajar al-Haytami, Musnad Ahmad bin Hanbal, Khasa'is Amir al-Mu'minin by Al-Nasa'i, Al-Mustadrak by Al-Hakim al-Naysaburi

## Biographical Dossiers (5)

- **Abu Zayd al-Ansari (Sa'id b. Aws)** — Highly positive. Evaluated as a supreme authority in the Arabic language, a reliable transmitter of Hadith, and one of the most knowledgeable linguists of Basra. Sources confirm he memorized two-thirds of the Arabic language, making him superior to Al-Asma'i and Abu Ubaydah in breadth of linguistic knowledge.
  Sources: Tahdhib al-Asma wa al-Lughat, Al-Ibar, Mir'at al-Janan, Ghayat al-Nihayah, Bughyat al-Wu'at, Al-Muzhir
- **Al-Farra (Yahya b. Ziyad)** — Highly positive. Evaluated as the absolute leader of the Kufan school of grammar, a brilliant scholar of literature, and the author of foundational texts in Arabic linguistics.
  Sources: Wafayat al-A'yan, Mir'at al-Janan, Tadhkirat al-Huffaz, Al-Ibar, Tarikh Baghdad
- **Abu Ubaydah (Ma'mar b. al-Muthanna)** — Highly positive. Evaluated as a master linguist, historian, and the very first scholar to compile a book on 'Gharib al-Hadith' (rare words in Hadith), making his linguistic interpretations highly authoritative.
  Sources: Tadhkirat al-Huffaz, Al-Ibar, Al-Nihayah fi Gharib al-Hadith, Al-Muzhir
- **Ibn al-Anbari (Muhammad b. al-Qasim)** — Highly positive. Evaluated as an Imam of literature and grammar, possessing an extraordinary memory (memorizing 300,000 verses of poetry as Quranic evidence), and a pious, reliable Sunni scholar.
  Sources: Ansab al-Sam'ani, Wafayat al-A'yan, Tadhkirat al-Huffaz, Mir'at al-Janan, Ghayat al-Nihayah, Bughyat al-Wu'at
- **Al-Zamakhshari (Mahmud b. Umar)** — Highly positive. Evaluated as the Imam of his era in Tafsir, Arabic grammar, and rhetoric, whose work 'Al-Kashshaf' is considered a pinnacle of linguistic exegesis.
  Sources: Kata'ib A'lam al-Akhyar, Jami' al-Usul, Mir'at al-Janan

# Abaqat al-Anwar — Volume 18 Response Analysis

**Hadith defended:** Hadith of Thaqalayn
**Section:** Sanad
**Analyzed by:** Gemini 3.1 Pro

## Overview
This volume focuses on proving the mass-transmitted (Mutawatir) status of Hadith al-Thaqalayn through Sunni sources. The author systematically dismantles Dehlavi's attempt to downplay the hadith. Dehlavi had claimed that the Shia rely solely on Zayd bin Arqam's narration and deliberately omitted key phrases like 'itrati' (my progeny) and 'lan yatafarraqa' (they shall not separate) from his presentation of the hadith to weaken the Shia argument for the Imamate and Ismah of the Ahl al-Bayt. In response, the author catalogs 187 prominent Sunni scholars across 12 centuries who narrated the hadith with the exact wording that supports the Shia doctrine, providing extensive biographical dossiers (tarajim) from Sunni Rijal books to prove the absolute reliability of these narrators.

## Responses to Dehlavi (2)

### Response 1: Dehlavi accepts the hadith but argues Sunnis are its true practitioners, and attempts to weaken the Shia argument by implying the hadith is an isolated report primarily from Zayd bin Arqam, omitting its Mutawatir status.
**Type:** SANAD_DEFENSE
**Author's response:** The author proves the hadith is Mutawatir by listing 187 Sunni scholars from the 2nd to the 13th century who narrated it. He shows it was narrated not just by Zayd bin Arqam, but also by Abu Sa'id al-Khudri, Zayd bin Thabit, Jabir bin 'Abdullah, Hudhayfah bin Asid, and 'Ali bin Abi Talib.
**Evidence:** Lists of narrators categorized by century, starting from Sa'id bin Masruq al-Thawri (d. 126 AH) up to scholars of the 13th century.

### Response 2: Dehlavi omitted the word 'itrati' (my progeny) and the phrase 'lan yatafarraqa hatta yarida 'alayya al-hawd' (they shall not separate until they meet me at the Pond) from his citation of the hadith to avoid the implication of the Ahl al-Bayt's infallibility.
**Type:** CONTRADICTION_EXPOSURE
**Author's response:** The author exposes this academic dishonesty (takhdimat) by quoting the exact texts from major Sunni Hadith books, demonstrating that these specific phrases are integral, authentic parts of the hadith as recorded by top Sunni authorities.
**Evidence:** Sahih Muslim, Sahih Tirmidhi, Musnad Ahmad bin Hanbal, Al-Mustadrak 'ala al-Sahihayn

## Biographical Dossiers (5)

- **'Abd al-Malik bin Abi Sulayman al-'Arzami** — Highly positive. Evaluated as a major Hafiz and one of the best scholars of Kufa. Ibn Hajar and Dhahabi defend him against isolated criticisms regarding his memory, establishing him as a reliable proof (Hujjah).
  Sources: Al-Thiqat (Ibn Hibban), Asma' Rijal al-Sahihayn (Ibn Tahir), Al-Ansab (Al-Sam'ani), Al-Kamal ('Abd al-Ghani al-Maqdisi), Tadhkirat al-Huffaz (Dhahabi), Tahdhib al-Tahdhib (Ibn Hajar)
- **Sulayman bin Mihran al-A'mash** — Extremely positive. Described as a massive pillar of Hadith, a 'Sign of Islam', and the most knowledgeable of his time in Kufa regarding Quran and Fara'id. Universally accepted as a top-tier reliable narrator.
  Sources: Al-Thiqat (Ibn Hibban), Asma' Rijal al-Sahihayn (Ibn Tahir), Al-Ansab (Al-Sam'ani), Al-Kamal ('Abd al-Ghani al-Maqdisi), Wafayat al-A'yan (Ibn Khallikan), Tadhkirat al-Huffaz (Dhahabi), Tahdhib al-Tahdhib (Ibn Hajar), Tabaqat al-Huffaz (Suyuti)
- **Muhammad bin Ishaq bin Yasar al-Madani** — Positive. Acknowledged as the Imam of Maghazi (Prophetic biographies). The author cites multiple Sunni authorities defending him against Imam Malik's harsh criticism, proving his hadiths are considered 'Hasan' or 'Sahih' by major scholars like Ahmad bin Hanbal and Shu'bah.
  Sources: Al-Thiqat (Ibn Hibban), Tadhkirat Khawas al-Ummah (Sibt Ibn al-Jawzi), Wafayat al-A'yan (Ibn Khallikan), Tahdhib al-Kamal (Al-Mizzi), Al-Kashif (Dhahabi), Tabaqat al-Shafi'iyyah (Subki)
- **Abu 'Awanah Waddah bin 'Abdullah al-Yashkuri** — Positive. Evaluated as a reliable Hafiz with a very correct book. Scholars noted that while he might make mistakes if narrating from memory, his written records were impeccable.
  Sources: Asma' Rijal al-Sahihayn (Ibn Tahir), Tahdhib al-Kamal (Al-Mizzi), Tadhkirat al-Huffaz (Dhahabi), Taqrib al-Tahdhib (Ibn Hajar), Tabaqat al-Huffaz (Suyuti)
- **Muslim bin al-Hajjaj al-Naysaburi** — Extremely positive. Universally recognized as the author of the Sahih, a pillar of Islam, and one of the greatest Hadith masters in history.
  Sources: Wafayat al-A'yan (Ibn Khallikan), Tadhkirat al-Huffaz (Dhahabi), Al-'Ibar (Dhahabi), Mir'at al-Janan (Yafi'i), Tabaqat al-Huffaz (Suyuti)

## Argument Lineages Traced (1)

- **The continuous, mass-transmitted (Mutawatir) status of Hadith al-Thaqalayn across all generations of Sunni scholarship.**
  2nd Century Scholars (e.g., Sa'id bin Masruq, al-A'mash, Isra'il bin Yunus)
  3rd Century Scholars (e.g., Ahmad bin Hanbal, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi)

  5th Century Scholars (e.g., Al-Hakim, Al-Bayhaqi, Ibn 'Abd al-Barr)
  Subsequent centuries up to the 13th Century

## Prior Rebuttals Cited (2)

- **Ibn Hajar al-'Asqalani** in *Tahdhib al-Tahdhib*: Refuted isolated criticisms against narrators like 'Abd al-Malik bin Abi Sulayman by citing the consensus of major Hadith masters (like Ahmad and Yahya bin Ma'in) who declared him a reliable proof.
- **Ibn Hibban** in *Al-Thiqat*: Defended Muhammad bin Ishaq against the harsh attacks of Malik bin Anas, explaining the personal reasons behind the conflict and affirming Ibn Ishaq's reliability in Hadith.
